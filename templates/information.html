DIABETES

"Control diabetes, don't let diabetes control you."

What is Diabetes?

Diabetes is a condition that happens when your blood sugar (glucose) is too high. It develops when your pancreas doesn’t make enough insulin or any at all, or when your body isn’t responding to the effects of insulin properly. Diabetes affects people of all ages. Most forms of diabetes are chronic (lifelong), and all forms are manageable with medications and/or lifestyle changes.

Glucose (sugar) mainly comes from carbohydrates in your food and drinks. It’s your body’s go-to source of energy. Your blood carries glucose to all your body’s cells to use for energy.

When glucose is in your bloodstream, it needs help — a “key” — to reach its final destination. This key is insulin (a hormone). If your pancreas isn’t making enough insulin or your body isn’t using it properly, glucose builds up in your bloodstream, causing high blood sugar (hyperglycemia).

Over time, having consistently high blood glucose can cause health problems, such as heart disease, nerve damage and eye issues.

The technical name for diabetes is diabetes mellitus. Another condition shares the term “diabetes” — diabetes insipidus — but they’re distinct. They share the name “diabetes” because they both cause increased thirst and frequent urination. Diabetes insipidus is much rarer than diabetes mellitus.

What are the types of diabetes?

There are several types of diabetes. The most common forms include:

  • Type 2 Diabetes: With this type, your body doesn’t make enough insulin and/or your body’s cells don’t respond normally to the insulin (insulin resistance). This is the most common type of diabetes. It mainly affects adults, but children can have it as well.
  • Prediabetes: This type is the stage before Type 2 diabetes. Your blood glucose levels are higher than normal but not high enough to be officially diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes.
  • Type 1 Diabetes: This type is an autoimmune disease in which your immune system attacks and destroys insulin-producing cells in your pancreas for unknown reasons. Up to 10% of people who have diabetes have Type 1. It’s usually diagnosed in children and young adults, but it can develop at any age.
  • Gestational Diabetes: This type develops in some people during pregnancy. Gestational diabetes usually goes away after pregnancy. However, if you have gestational diabetes, you’re at a higher risk of developing Type 2 diabetes later in life.

    • Other types of diabetes include:

  • Type 3c Diabetes: This form of diabetes happens when your pancreas experiences damage (other than autoimmune damage), which affects its ability to produce insulin. Pancreatitis, pancreatic cancer, cystic fibrosis and hemochromatosis can all lead to pancreas damage that causes diabetes. Having your pancreas removed (pancreatectomy) also results in Type 3c.
  • Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults (LADA): Like Type 1 diabetes, LADA also results from an autoimmune reaction, but it develops much more slowly than Type 1. People diagnosed with LADA are usually over the age of 30.
  • Maturity-onset Diabetes of the Young (MODY): MODY, also called monogenic diabetes, happens due to an inherited genetic mutation that affects how your body makes and uses insulin. There are currently over 10 different types of MODY. It affects up to 5% of people with diabetes and commonly runs in families.
  • Neonatal Diabetes: This is a rare form of diabetes that occurs within the first six months of life. It’s also a form of monogenic diabetes. About 50% of babies with neonatal diabetes have the lifelong form called permanent neonatal diabetes mellitus. For the other half, the condition disappears within a few months from onset, but it can come back later in life. This is called transient neonatal diabetes mellitus.
  • Brittle Diabetes: Brittle diabetes is a form of Type 1 diabetes that’s marked by frequent and severe episodes of high and low blood sugar levels. This instability often leads to hospitalization. In rare cases, a pancreas transplant may be necessary to permanently treat brittle diabetes.

How common is diabetes?

Diabetes is common. Approximately 37.3 million people in the United States have diabetes, which is about 11% of the population. Type 2 diabetes is the most common form, representing 90% to 95% of all diabetes cases.

About 537 million adults across the world have diabetes. Experts predict this number will rise to 643 million by 2030 and 783 million by 2045.

What are the risk factors for heart disease?

High blood pressure, high blood cholesterol, and smoking are key risk factors for heart disease. About half of people in the United States (47%) have at least one of these three risk factors.2 Several other medical conditions and lifestyle choices can also put people at a higher risk for heart disease, including

  • Diabetes
  • Overweight and obesity
  • Unhealthy diet
  • Physical inactivity
  • Excessive alcohol use

Your healthcare provider can treat PCOS based on your symptoms and if you wish to become pregnant. Coronary artery disease (CAD) is the most common form of heart disease. It occurs when the arteries that supply blood to the heart muscle become narrowed or blocked due to the buildup of plaque, a fatty substance. This can restrict blood flow to the heart, leading to chest pain (angina), heart attacks, or other complications.

Heart failure occurs when the heart is unable to pump blood effectively, resulting in inadequate blood supply to meet the body's needs. It can be caused by conditions such as CAD, high blood pressure, or heart muscle damage.

How common is heart disease?

Heart disease kills more people in the United States than any other disease. In 2020, heart disease caused 1 in 5 deaths. That’s nearly 700,000 people. Coronary artery disease, the most common type of heart disease, caused about 380,000 of those deaths.

Heart disease is a significant global health concern and one of the leading causes of death worldwide. Its prevalence varies among countries and populations, but it affects millions of people globally. Here are some key statistics regarding the prevalence of heart disease:

Global Burden: According to the World Health Organization (WHO), cardiovascular diseases, including heart disease, account for nearly 18 million deaths worldwide each year, representing 31% of all global deaths.

Leading Cause of Death: Heart disease is the leading cause of death in many countries, including the United States, Europe, and most developed nations. It surpasses other major causes such as cancer, respiratory diseases, and infectious diseases.

Regional Variations: The prevalence of heart disease can vary significantly across regions. Developed countries tend to have higher rates of heart disease due to factors like aging populations, sedentary lifestyles, unhealthy diets, and higher rates of obesity and smoking.

Risk Factors: The increasing prevalence of heart disease is closely linked to risk factors such as high blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, obesity, diabetes, and unhealthy lifestyle choices. These risk factors are often modifiable through preventive measures and lifestyle changes.

Impact on Developing Countries: While heart disease has historically been associated with developed countries, it is also becoming a major concern in developing nations. Rapid urbanization, changes in lifestyle, and shifts in dietary habits contribute to an increased risk of heart disease in these regions.

How does heart disease affect my body?

When your heart isn’t working well, it has trouble sending enough blood to your body. Your blood brings oxygen and nutrients to your body’s organs, tissues and cells. If they can’t get what they need, it’s harder for them to function. In a way, your heart delivers the fuel that keeps your body’s systems running. If there’s a problem with delivering that fuel, it affects everything your body’s systems do.

Heart disease can have significant impacts on the body, affecting various organs and bodily functions. Here are some ways in which heart disease can affect the body:

Reduced Blood Flow: Heart disease, such as coronary artery disease, can lead to narrowed or blocked arteries, reducing blood flow to the heart muscle. This can cause symptoms such as chest pain (angina) or shortness of breath, particularly during physical exertion.

Impaired Heart Function: Heart disease can weaken the heart muscle, leading to impaired pumping ability. This can result in heart failure, where the heart cannot adequately supply the body's organs and tissues with oxygenated blood. Symptoms of heart failure include fatigue, swelling in the legs and ankles, and difficulty breathing.

Arrhythmias: Certain heart diseases can disrupt the heart's electrical system, causing abnormal heart rhythms or arrhythmias. This can result in a rapid heartbeat (tachycardia), slow heartbeat (bradycardia), or irregular heartbeat. Arrhythmias can cause palpitations, dizziness, fainting, and in severe cases, can lead to complications like stroke or cardiac arrest.

Increased Risk of Blood Clots: Heart disease can increase the risk of blood clots forming within the blood vessels. Blood clots can partially or completely block blood flow, leading to serious complications such as heart attack or stroke.

Organ Damage: Inadequate blood supply due to heart disease can affect various organs and tissues, leading to their dysfunction or damage. Organs such as the kidneys, liver, and lungs may be particularly vulnerable to the reduced blood flow and oxygenation.

What is Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome?

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is a hormonal imbalance that occurs when your ovaries (the organ that produces and releases eggs) create excess hormones. If you have PCOS, your ovaries produce unusually high levels of hormones called androgens. This causes your reproductive hormones to become imbalanced. As a result, people with PCOS often have irregular menstrual cycles, missed periods and unpredictable ovulation. Small follicle cysts (fluid-filled sacs with immature eggs) may be visible on your ovaries on ultrasound due to lack of ovulation (anovulation). However, despite the name "polycystic," you don’t need to have cysts on your ovaries to have PCOS. The ovarian cysts aren’t dangerous or painful.

PCOS is one of the most common causes of infertility in women and people assigned female at birth (AFAB). It can also increase your risk of other health conditions. Your healthcare provider can treat PCOS based on your symptoms and if you wish to become pregnant.

What age does PCOS start?

Women and people AFAB can get PCOS any time after puberty. Most people are diagnosed in their 20s or 30s when they’re trying to get pregnant. You may have a higher chance of getting PCOS if you have obesity or if other people in your biological family have PCOS. PCOS typically emerges between the ages of 15 and 30, with most women being diagnosed in their late teens or early twenties. However, PCOS can also occur in adolescence, and some girls may experience signs and symptoms as early as their first menstrual period.

It is important to note that the diagnosis of PCOS is not solely based on age but requires the presence of specific criteria. These criteria include irregular menstrual cycles, elevated levels of male hormones (androgens), and the presence of polycystic ovaries on ultrasound. Additionally, other symptoms such as acne, excessive hair growth (hirsutism), weight gain, and fertility issues may also be present.

PCOS is a complex hormonal disorder, and its exact cause is not fully understood. It is believed to involve a combination of genetic, environmental, and hormonal factors. While the symptoms may start during adolescence or early adulthood, PCOS can persist throughout a woman's reproductive years and may continue to pose challenges related to menstrual irregularities, fertility issues, and metabolic health.

If you suspect you may have PCOS or are experiencing symptoms associated with the condition, it is recommended to consult with a healthcare professional who can evaluate your symptoms, perform appropriate diagnostic tests, and provide guidance on management and treatment options.

How common is PCOS?

PCOS is very common — up to 15% of women and people AFAB of reproductive age have PCOS.

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is a common hormonal disorder that affects a significant number of women of reproductive age. Here are some key statistics regarding the prevalence of PCOS:

Global Prevalence: The exact prevalence of PCOS varies across different populations and geographical regions. According to estimates by the World Health Organization (WHO), PCOS affects 5% to 15% of women worldwide.

Most Common Endocrine Disorder in Women: PCOS is recognized as the most common endocrine disorder in women of reproductive age. It is a leading cause of menstrual irregularities, infertility, and hormonal imbalances.

Underdiagnosed Condition: Despite its prevalence, PCOS remains underdiagnosed in many cases. Some women may experience symptoms without seeking medical attention or receiving a proper diagnosis. Lack of awareness among both patients and healthcare providers contributes to underdiagnosis.

Impact on Fertility: PCOS is a significant cause of female infertility. It is estimated that 70% to 80% of women with PCOS experience ovulation-related infertility. The condition can affect the regular release of eggs from the ovaries, leading to difficulties in conception.

Associated Health Risks: PCOS is often associated with various metabolic and cardiovascular risks. Women with PCOS have a higher likelihood of developing insulin resistance, obesity, type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, dyslipidemia (abnormal lipid levels), and cardiovascular disease. These risks highlight the importance of managing PCOS comprehensively and addressing related health concerns.

Impact on Quality of Life: PCOS can significantly impact a woman's quality of life due to the physical and emotional symptoms it presents. Symptoms such as menstrual irregularities, excessive hair growth (hirsutism), acne, weight gain, and mood disturbances can affect self-esteem, body image, and overall well-being.
